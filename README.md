# HR Attrition Analysis Dashboard

## Overview
The HR Attrition Analysis dashboard provides insights into employee turnover within the organization. With a total workforce of **780 employees** (455 male and 325 female), the organization recorded **234 cases of attrition**, resulting in an attrition rate of approximately **30%**. The analysis highlights key factors such as gender, age, job role, department, years of service, and salary that influence attrition.

## Tools Used

- **Excel**: For data cleaning and preparation.
- **Power BI**: For creating the interactive dashboard and visualizations.
  
![HR Attrition Dashboard](https://github.com/Josh-Osas/HR-Attrition-Analytics/blob/main/HR%20Attrition%20Dashboard.png)

## Key Insights

1. **Attrition by Gender**:
   - **Female Employees**: 111 employees (47.44% of total attrition).
   - **Male Employees**: 123 employees (52.56% of total attrition).
   - **Insight**: Male employees exhibit a slightly higher attrition rate compared to female employees, suggesting potential areas for improvement in male employee retention strategies.


2. **Attrition by Age**:
   - **Insight**: The majority of attrition occurs in employees aged **30 to 40 years**, with a noticeable peak in the early 30s. Attrition significantly declines after the age of 40, indicating a stable workforce among more experienced employees.


3. **Attrition by Salary**:
   - **Insight**: Attrition is more prominent among employees earning between **$50,000 to $70,000** annually. Employees earning **below $40,000 or above $80,000** exhibit lower attrition rates, suggesting that salary packages in this middle range might need further review or adjustment.


4. **Attrition by Job Role**:
   - **Insight**: **Specialists** experience the highest attrition, followed by **Engineers** and **Analysts**. **Managers, Sales Representatives, and Accountants** show relatively lower attrition rates. Female attrition is notably higher among Specialists and Analysts, while male attrition is more significant in Engineering.


5. **Attrition by Years of Service**:
   - **Insight**: Employees with **2 to 6 years of service** are most likely to leave, indicating challenges in retaining **mid-tenure employees**. Attrition drops significantly after 10 years of service, suggesting higher retention among long-serving employees.


6. **Average Satisfaction and Performance**:
   - **Average Satisfaction Score**: 3.78 (out of 5), indicating room for improvement in employee engagement and morale.
   - **Average Performance Score**: 6.80 (out of 10), suggesting moderate performance levels among employees.


## Recommendations

1. **Enhance Employee Engagement and Satisfaction**:
   - Conduct regular employee surveys to identify areas of dissatisfaction and implement targeted improvements.
   - Offer professional development opportunities to boost morale and retention, especially for mid-tenure employees.

2. **Tailored Retention Strategies**:
   - Focus on reducing attrition in high-risk groups such as **Specialists**, **Engineers**, and **Analysts**.
   - Develop customized retention plans for female employees, particularly in **HR** and **Specialist roles**.

3. **Salary Review and Incentives**:
   - Address attrition trends in the **$50,000 to $70,000 salary range** by introducing competitive compensation packages or performance-based incentives.

4. **Strengthen Onboarding and Early Career Support**:
   - Provide robust training and mentorship programs for employees in their first **five years of service** to foster long-term commitment.

5. **Support Work-Life Balance**:
   - Introduce policies and initiatives to support employees in their early 30s, such as flexible work arrangements or wellness programs.

6. **Proactive Succession Planning**:
   - Identify high-potential employees in critical roles and create **succession plans** to mitigate the impact of attrition.


## Conclusion

This analysis underscores the need for **targeted interventions** to address the high attrition rates among specific demographics and job roles. By prioritizing employee satisfaction, addressing compensation concerns, and fostering a supportive work environment, the organization can reduce attrition and build a more stable workforce.
